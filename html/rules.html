Rules of Wisconsin Chess

Chess Variation

Wisconsin Chess is a chess variation. If you know chess then much of this game will be familiar. If you don't know chess then you will learn it by playing Wisconsin Chess.

Players

Chess is a board game played between two people across from each other. One player controls the white army and the other player controls the black army.

The person controlling the white army makes the first move. Chess is played as turns, so a player makes a move then the other player makes a move which is repeated until the game is completed.

Board

The board is a grid of squares with eight rows and eight columns. Each person's army begins on the 16 squares of the two rows closest to them.

Each army is a set of pieces with varying ability. Each kind of piece has a name. The front row closer to the opponent is all pawns and the back row has a variety.

Moving Pieces

Every piece in the army can either move to empty squares or move to squares with an opponent piece. Each piece has a known set of moves it's allowed to do.

When a move is onto an opponent's piece then the move is called a capture. Capturing a piece takes it off the board into the possession of the capturing player. Captured pieces no longer affect the game.

The Kings

The object of chess is to capture your opponent's king piece. This is called a checkmate when it happens.

"Mary won against Patricia with an unexpected checkmate early in the game."

If a king could be captured the next turn then the king is said to be in check. Any of your moves that cause your king to become in check are illegal. If your king is in check then the move you make must cause the king to leave check.

The idea of moving into check has some nuance. Check is a threat of capture, which is different than a move to capture. Opponent moves that would normally be illegal because it would move their king into check are still considered threats when determining if your king is being moved into check.

"Jennifer has put Linda in check. There's only two moves that can be made, which should Linda choose?"

Checkmate is when a player's king cannot leave check. If a king is not in check but every move is into check then the game is a draw.

Other Draws

Besides that case of not being in check but having no moves available, there are other reasons why a game can be declared a draw.

If no pawns remain and the pieces on the board are too few to cause a checkmate then the game is a draw.

If no pawn has moved and no captures have happened for a large number of turns then the game is a draw.

Wisconsin Chess Additions to Chess

Wisconsin Chess adds special pieces that replace the regular chess pieces. Each special piece is based on a regular piece and can only be placed onto an army square that matches the basic kind it is.

Each player of Wisconsin Chess has a collection that has up to 21 special pieces. You can place these into your army before starting a game. The collection stays the same unless you decide to replace a piece.

A special piece is presented as a reward for completing a game. If you want it then you place the piece into your collection.

When customizing your army before a match you are presented with two "random pick" pieces. These two slots are two random special pieces that stay the same until used in your army then added to your collection when the game is completed. If a random pick is added to the collection then it's randomly replaced with another special piece.

Each piece in Wisconsin Chess has zero, one, or two characteristics that cause added effects. These are named on the details webpage for each piece.

Some pieces, like the simple, convey temporary changes to the characteristics of other pieces. These changes apply throughout the move, even if an affected piece is moved away from the piece affecting it. This matters in situations such as if a knight is adjacent to a simple then is moved away into range of a constructive which asserts and causes the neutralize effect without being in range of the ordering piece.

The interactions of piece characteristics can cause surprising results. I think the best way to enjoy Wisconsin Chess is to play many games and only read characteristic and piece details for a quick overview or as a response to specific situations you were surprised by. To keep the game similar to chess these characteristics are deterministic (a move always has the same result) which means their specification can be more complicated than is needed usually.

Thanks for playing Wisconsin Chess, and have fun!
